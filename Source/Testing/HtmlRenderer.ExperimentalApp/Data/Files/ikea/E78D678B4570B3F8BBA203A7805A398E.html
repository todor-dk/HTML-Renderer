      My first IKEA experience was in the Plymouth Meeting, PA store over 30 years ago. This new store has some issues: 1). If it's 60°F outside, it seems it's over 80°F inside the store. 2). The building vibrates, whether you're standing on the concrete floors or out on the elevated parking decks you can feel it. There's likely nothing that can be done about issue 2, but they need to seriously upgrade their AC to make this a hospitable place for shopper and employee alike. On a busy day this places feels a bit like a sauna, and that's not conducive to shopping. Now, we could discuss furniture quality ad nauseam, but suffice to say this is, at best, starter furniture for your first apartment or a college dorm. Maybe a lightly used guest bedroom or a sunroom. Prices have seem to gone way up on many categories, and the values I recall from years back have largely gone away, although you can still find some, but far fewer than what once was. I usually find I have a great sense of direction, but this store can be a confusing mess. Just follow the arrows to feel a bit less like a rat in a hot maze. Checkouts have always been impossibly slow, but anyone who has been to an iKEA already knows you are going to need some serious patience. What you may not know is that you may also need to bring a bathing suit here to feel comfortable in this massive, vibrating sweat lodge.

You can only call a general customer service number where you'll speak to somebody whom may or may not actually get in touch with the store on your behalf. In my case, it was a not. Guess who didn't hear back from IKEA within the next two days? Me. Once it was obvious that nobody from IKEA was going to be reaching out to us, my husband called the same horrible customer service number to find out what was going on. It was now the 21st (Wednesday), and he was told that our information was never sent over to the store. The man he spoke to took down all of our information and told my husband that we would hear back from IKEA within the next two days. We didn't. Now it was back around to my turn to deal with calling IKEA's customer service. It was Saturday, September 24th. An entire week after we had initially purchased the shelf. This time I was told that our missing box would be sent out on Monday and that we would have it by Thursday at the latest. I was hopeful. I was naive. I should have known something was up when I asked for a confirmation/tracking number and was told that one wasn't available. So, unsurprisingly, Thursday came and went, and we didn't received our 4th box. Back around to my husband's turn to call. It was September 29th and he was told that the package was never sent out, but that it would be this time. We were told that the box would be sent out that weekend and that a confirmation number would be sent to my email within the next day or so. We went the whole weekend without receiving anything, so we called on Monday (October 3rd) to check up on things. We had a half built shelving unit and stuff all over our apartment. We wanted our shelf, damn it. At this point we were finally given a confirmation number stating that we had two day delivery, that our package would be shipped out that day, and that we would have it on Wednesday (October 5th). Huzzah! Our missing box actually showed up this time. Sadly though, it wasn't until Thursday (October 6th), and it had the horrible, shelvy nerve to show up damaged/peeling. However, we just couldn't wait any longer. We needed to finish putting this thing together. So, we took the peeling shelf and put it up in our apartment. The clutter was driving us mad. We did what we had to do. Days elapsed since initial Kallax Shelving Unit purchase? 19. Days until I forgive IKEA? Unknown.

      I've been to a dozen or so Ikea locations up and down the east coast, including my visit to this location yesterday. One thing is for sure, one is not better than the rest. They all offer the same stock, are about the same size, and have the same set up - displays upstairs, inventory downstairs. In the 20 + years I've been a customer it's been a love/hate affair. LOVE the displays providing room design ideas, the multiple ways they use the same pieces, and the fact that you can see and feel how each piece functions. Love the options and choices. Love the prices and abundance of inventory, the way things are packed to make transporting home in a car easy. Love that they also have Swedish food to eat there or take home prepackaged. HATE the return process (if you can even get them to take something back), the hunt for the merchandise on your list, the complex assembly with limited instructions. Hate the poor quality and the fact that inventory and parts change often and chances are if something breaks you can't get replacement parts. Hate the long lines that are present at every location, the lack of help loading. Hate that you have to buy their bag if you want your items bagged. One thing I have discovered is even if you scour the catalog for days on end, make a list and check it twice once you get to the store all will change. You'll see a piece in a display different from the catalog and fall in love, or the opposite - you'll see the piece you came for and the display isn't how you envisioned. Either way sometimes too many choices equal too much frustration. This coming from a woman that once packed up my car, measured the space remaining, and went back and purchased more. That drove home two hours down the turnpike smack dab against the windshield because the driver's seat held not only myself but a box. Or that I once loaded my car so full I couldn't even see my children. Yes, all this has happened and happens all the time. If you want a good comical reality experience just sit in the parking lot and watch people load for a few hours.... Overall as I aged so did my style. While the catalog and displays are nice, rarely if ever does that same piece look like that in my own home. My daughter's bedroom was entirely Ikea and in less than a year we had to do multiple repairs on our own. Drawer bottoms are week, side rails are plastic, beds and dressers are no more than particleboard wrapped in a laminate skin. Wheels on trundle beds are undersized and the design faulty. What works for the Swedes unfortunately doesn't work for American children. As a middle aged woman I now come for the inspiration. I come for the accessories, the candles in bulk, and the furniture pieces that are inexpensive and will only get occasional use. I come for the unique - things that can only be found at Ikea. For a day browsing with girlfriends or killing time in a vacation location. So this love affair for the most part is over, or maybe just categorized to friends with benefits.

        I've been to a dozen or so Ikea locations up and down the east coast, including my visit to this location yesterday. One thing is for sure, one is not better than the rest.  They all offer the same stock, are about the same size, and have the same set up - displays upstairs, inventory downstairs.  In the 20 + years I've been a customer it's been a love/hate affair.

        LOVE the displays providing room design ideas, the multiple ways they use the same pieces, and the fact that you can see and feel how each piece functions.  Love the options and choices.  Love the prices and abundance of inventory, the way things are packed to make transporting home in a car easy.  Love that they also have Swedish food to eat there or take home prepackaged.

        HATE the return process (if you can even get them to take something back), the hunt for the merchandise on your list, the complex assembly with limited instructions.  Hate the poor quality and the fact that inventory and parts change often and chances are if something breaks you can't get replacement parts.  Hate the long lines that are present at every location, the lack of help loading.  Hate that you have to buy their bag if you want your items bagged.

        One thing I have discovered is even if you scour the catalog for days on end, make a list and check it twice once you get to the store all will change.  You'll see a piece in a display different from the catalog and fall in love, or the opposite - you'll see the piece you came for and the display isn't how you envisioned.  Either way sometimes too many choices equal too much frustration.

        This coming from a woman that once packed up my car, measured the space remaining, and went back and purchased more.  That drove home two hours down the turnpike smack dab against the windshield because the driver's seat held not only myself but a box.  Or that I once loaded my car so full I couldn't even see my children.  Yes, all this has happened and happens all the time.  If you want a good comical reality experience just sit in the parking lot and watch people load for a few hours....

        Overall as I aged so did my style.  While the catalog and displays are nice, rarely if ever does that same piece look like that in my own home.  My daughter's bedroom was entirely Ikea and in less than a year we had to do multiple repairs on our own.  Drawer bottoms are week, side rails are plastic, beds and dressers are no more than particleboard wrapped in a laminate skin.  Wheels on trundle beds are undersized and the design faulty.  What works for the Swedes unfortunately doesn't work for American children.

        As a middle aged woman I now come for the inspiration.  For ideas that I can take home and rework with more structurally sound pieces.  I come for the accessories, the candles in bulk, and the furniture pieces that are inexpensive and will only get occasional use.  I come for the unique - things that can only be found at Ikea.  For a day browsing with girlfriends or killing time in a vacation location.

        So this love  affair for the most part is over, or maybe just categorized to friends with benefits.
