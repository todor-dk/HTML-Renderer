What parents need to know

Educational value

WhatsApp Messenger wasn't created with educational intent, and we don't recommend it for learning.

Ease of play

WhatsApp works seamlessly among most types of smartphones. The only requirement for phones to send and receive messages is that both users have installed this app and that they have each other as contacts in their phones. A green check mark on the phone indicates the message has been sent, and two check marks indicate the person has viewed or listened to the message. Even when WhatsApp isn't open, push notifications will alert you when you have a new message. An extensive FAQ answers most questions about how to use general features and potential problems.

Violence

No violent content in the app itself, but teens can send and receive unmonitored texts, photos, voice messages, and videos.

Sex

No sexual content in the app itself, but teens can send and receive unmonitored texts, photos, voice messages, and videos.

Language

No iffy language in the app itself, but teens can send and receive unmonitored texts, photos, voice messages, and videos.

Consumerism

What'sApp Messenger encourages users to allow it to send a message to everyone in their address books about signing up for the service, since you can only message those who also have this app. It's free for the first year and then automatically charges $0.99 a year after that.

Drinking, drugs, & smoking

No content related to drinking, drugs, or smoking in the app itself, but teens can send and receive unmonitored texts, photos, voice messages, and videos.

Parents Need to Know

Parents need to know that WhatsApp Messenger lets users start video calls and send encrypted text messages, videos, photos, and audio messages to one or many people with no message limits or fees until after the first year of use, when there's a $0.99 annual fee. The catch is these messages can only be sent to other smartphone users who also have WhatsApp. Once you install the app, it checks your address book to see if anyone else you know is already using WhatsApp and connects you automatically. The app also suggests allowing messages to be sent from your phone to those in your address book who aren't yet using WhatsApp, encouraging them to sign up. WhatsApp's license agreement requires users to be age 16 or older. In terms of questionable content, it's completely up to the user, but location-sharing capabilities make this app most appropriate for older teens. As of 2016 WhatsApp shares data with parent company Facebook; though users can opt out of some of the data sharing for a limited time, they can't completely opt out. Read the app's privacy policy to find out more about the types of information collected and shared.

What's it about?

WHATSAPP MESSENGER is among the messaging apps that use push notifications to allow users to message each other without the limits set for texting in typical mobile phone plans. Simply select someone from your phone's contact list who also has WhatsApp and tap on the message icon. Then type the message, press and hold the microphone icon to send a voice message, or tap the attachment icon to send a photo, video, or location update. Users can also start video calls, similar to FaceTime. A user could literally "send a million messages a day" and not be charged more than the $0.99 annual fee (after the first free year). In 2016 the developers rolled out end-to-end encryption, which means no information from any type of message is stored or accessible on the company's servers, and messages are private during transmission, though they can be recovered from your device unless you delete the app entirely. They also started sharing data with parent company Facebook, and only a partial opt-out is allowed for a limited time.

Is it any good?

QUALITY

This app has a lot of great features, but many parents may find the idea of no limits on messaging -- as well as location sharing and constant status updates for teens -- unappealing, at best. For other parents, whose teens have run up bills going over a limited texting plan or who contact friends and family internationally, this app may be especially useful. The voice-messaging feature's impressively clear audio is a plus, as are group texting and check marks that show when recipients open each message. 

Families can talk about...

  • Families can talk about limits with WhatsApp Messenger and other apps: During what hours are they allowed to text? How much screen time are they allowed?

  • Talk about the types of information that are best shared in text messages and what is best discussed in person. Let kids know that tone isn't always clear, and emojis can't fill in every blank.
