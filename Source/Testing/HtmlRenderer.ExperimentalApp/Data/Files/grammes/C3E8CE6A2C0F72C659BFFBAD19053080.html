Atrial fibrillation (also called a-fib) is a heart condition that causes an irregular or abnormally fast heartbeat.

When at rest, the heart normally beats between 60 and 100 times per minute. A person with atrial fibrillation may have an unpredictable heart rate or a resting heart rate of over 140 beats per minute.

When the heart beats, the muscle walls around it contract, forcing blood out and around the body. When the muscles relax, the heart fills back up with blood. In atrial fibrillation, the upper chambers of the heart contract randomly. They often contract so fast that the heart muscles cannot relax properly between contractions. This reduces the heart’s efficiency and performance. Abnormal electrical impulses can start firing randomly, overriding the heart’s natural pacemaker, creating an irregular pulse rate.

While the exact cause of a-fib is unknown, it is more common in certain age groups and can be triggered by drinking or smoking. It commonly occurs in conjunction with other conditions such as high blood pressure, atherosclerosis, and heart conditions. Individuals with atrial fibrillation may not have any symptoms. Some people are aware of a change in heart rate or experience chest pain, dizziness, shortness of breath, fatigue, and palpations.

While the condition is not directly life-threatening, atrial fibrillation increases the risk of serious complications, including stroke and heart failure.

Paroxysmal atrial fibrillation may come and go and stops within 48 hours without treatment. Persistent atrial fibrillation may last as long as 7 days, depending on treatment. Longstanding persistent atrial fibrillation is continuous atrial fibrillation that occurs for more than a year. Permanent atrial fibrillation is the most severe form and is present at all times.

Treatment for atrial fibrillation varies depending on the person, the symptoms, and the severity of the condition. Treatment focuses on supporting comfort and preventing complications. Medication can be used to help prevent a stroke as well as control heart rate and heart rhythm. In some cases the heart can be given a controlled electrical shock to restore normal rhythm. In extreme cases a pacemaker can be surgically implanted to help the heart beat normally.
