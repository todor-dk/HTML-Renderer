      Indigo Prophecy / 6 Mar 2001

      Fahrenheit

      Share.
      Quantic Dream introduces a new episodic adventure title.
      By IGN Staff

      The guys in the Quantic Dream San Francisco offices are artists, and by artists I of course mean vague. Some fantastic ideas are flying out of the small loft space located in the heart of the SoMa district, but you'd have to listen close and follow even closer if you want to catch enough of it to assemble into a full logical concept. But that's ok -- they seem to have a head on what they're doing, and in terms of product, that's all that really matters. Right now, those French and European heads are wrapped around a new concept called Fahrenheit, an episodic game that the company hopes will launch a whole new series of adventure titles from the company that crafted the highly-stylized Omikron.

      The key to all this is the Interactive Cinematic Entertainment system (ICE for short), Quantic Dream's storytelling concept that's the foundation of the Fahrenheit experience. While it's nearly impossible to get them to tell you what ICE means on an literal level, it's something they've been working on for a while, and a system that they plan on using with other titles, beyond Fahrenheit. On a broad level it means that the game will be familiar to those that have played Omikron -- a mixture of movie cinematics, world interactivity, adventure gaming. It's an action/adventure hybrid of sorts, though the team says that they've learned a lot from the development of the original Omikron, specifically in trying to mix too many genres together in one product. So while you'll see a melding of action and adventure in Fahrenheit, don't expect to flip from first person shooting segments to racing games to a fighting engine in a series of minutes, something Omikron both excelled and fell flat on. Fahrenheit is much more story-focused, even moreso than Omikron, particularly to do with the episodic format in which the game will be released. But what exactly is Fahrenheit, anyway?

      Think of it as a realistic but paranormal thriller. Or think of it as NYPD Blue meets X-Files, in the words of the designers. Like Omikron, you'll focus on a few key characters, but will have the ability to play as a whole variety of "actors" in the story, even including the villain. You begin in the gutter, and it's your job to reach beyond human logic and into the realm of the supernatural by the time you finish your journey. The gutter, in this case, being the bathroom of a diner on the lower east side, where a murder has just happened. The only problem is, you've committed it. It's your job to play as Lucas Kain, the "hunted," and get yourself out of a bad situation before the cops help get you into a worse one. Of course, it's hard to take sides when you also play as the cops only a few moments later. Carla and Tyler are partners, and you'll have to help them in the investigation to find Kain... but is it really that easy? There's rumors of a dark priest involved, as well as some otherworldly happenings shaking up New York. Not to mention the fact that it's been getting unusually cold in the city, much colder than it should for the time of year. Over the course of 13 episodes, you'll play as all of these characters and more as you get to the heart of the mystery behind the city murders, the weather, and a mysterious little girl who seems to connect all of the event together in a macabre thread.

      Because it's episodic, the team is trying to ensure that the game is fit into 22-30 minute capsules of gaming, with "Expert Eggs," interactive secrets, that can boost the amount of playing time another 20 minutes. Think of the game as a sort of interactive television show, and you'll begin to grasp how things work. You'll have to solve puzzles and seek out clues, but a lot of it is also just pure storyline. QD's plan is to give you a variable amount of interaction, depending on how much you want to get involved with the game. If you're a spectator, you'll have more prompts on what to do next, and if you're an expert, than you'll have the option to search out the secrets and discover things for yourself. The team likes to call it "Bungie Stories," a way of allowing you to explore branches and sections of the game while still springing everything back to a neat conclusion in a short amount of time. Where Omikron tended to get lost as the story unraveled, Fahrenheit promises to keep the plotlines and gameplay short, sweet and always on track.

      More than any other aspect of gaming, Quantic Dream is particularly focused on the overall minute-by-minute experience of playing a game, and that's kept intact with this title as well. For instance, in the fourth episode of Fahrenheit you'll be able to pursue a certain aspect of the case with Carla and Tyler, or just go on a date and enjoy trying to "succeed" in your own personal social goals, whatever those may be. While it sounds silly to get excited over polygonal dating, just remember that only a few years ago, we were still just throwing bullets at demons and jumping on mushrooms with animated animals. As the industry gets more mature, you can expect to see more character development, and TV-style interaction than previously seen in games. For Sims fans, and those of us that loved the personal touches in The Longest Journey, it's another sign that the games, they are a changin'.

      In terms of technology, the game uses a fully 3D engine to represent the environment, with characters fleshed out in NERBs. Remember NERBs? Never mind. Anyway, Quantic Dream is at it again, with a whole slew of interactive objects in the environments. Basically, if you see it in 3D, you can usually interact with it. I wouldn't expect Shen Mue levels of bliss, but for those of you that enjoy clicking and looking, you'll be satisfied with the level of object interaction. In terms of interface, the current alpha setup has a Director's bar that controls a set of cameras that you can choose to view the scene in, as well as your standard save/load options. The Audience bar, however, is where the action is. You'll be able to interact with "Quicktime" events (another reference to Shen Mue) in this bar, as well as choose conversation branches, and emotion choices for certain situations that will change how the story progresses. Like Omikron, it looks to be a controllable with a keyboard and mouse.

      So what you've got, then, is an adventure game that plays out over the course of months, rather than hours, where you'll constantly be stuck with cliffhangers, and left to wonder what's around the corner, and what's going to happen with your favorite heroes. Only this time, you'll have to wait until next month's installment for the next chunk of the saga. What's most interesting about having the game play out in a series of episodes is that the audience will also have a part in deciding in where the story goes through polls and feedback. While this doesn't mean that the game is decided on the fly per se, it will allow the team to let gamers have a direct affect on certain aspects of the storyline. Right now the team is looking at October for a consumer launch of the series, considering it takes about two months to produce and episode, with a plan to release the series one month at a time. The one major question mark is the price -- details and partners are still being sorted out, so while the team is entertaining a variety of pay options, like the monthly fee planned for EA's Majestic, another episodic game, nothing has been finalized. That also goes for the platforms, as well. PC development is certainly the primary objective right now for Fahrenheit, but Quantic is well aware of the console potential, particularly given hard drives and online options that are being sported in next-generation consoles. Again, nothing is official, but QD wants to bring Fahrenheit to the largest audience possible, and is already working on how to get console controllers to work with the interface.

      Quantic Dream certainly knows the pitch, but can Fahrenheit deliver? The ideas and concepts are certainly interesting, but like any new leap, it's a matter of whether there's an audience, and if that audience will be interested in a melding of TV and gaming in such a fashion. Companies like EA and Arush are betting money on the fact that gamers and couch potatoes are looking for the next step, and are willing to stick their necks out on the line for it. Now Quantic Dreams has jumped into the fray as well. Episodic gaming is obviously the next step, now it's just a matter of who has the best walk.

      -- Vincent Lopez
