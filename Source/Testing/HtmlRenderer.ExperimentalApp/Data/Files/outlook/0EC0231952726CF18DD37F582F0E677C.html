St. Anthony leads Midtown revival

Published: April 16, 2017 12:00 AM CDT

Visiting Midtown today, it's easy to forget the neighborhood could have taken an entirely different direction in 2003 when St. Anthony Hospital contemplated a move to southwest Oklahoma City.

The historic Plaza Court building across the street from the hospital was empty and dark. Prostitutes and drug dealers frequented a motel nestled among the hospital's campus structures.

Boarded-up buildings, flop houses and dive bars had turned the neighborhood into a truly worthy candidate for a blight declaration.

Physicians were seeking to go to the suburbs, developers were offering incentives to the hospital to make the move, as well, and civic leaders were stunned to learn St. Anthony was indeed contemplating abandoning its historic home for a new $250 million campus.

Civic leaders, led by then-Mayor Kirk Humphreys, made their best pitch to the hospital to stick it out. The city and county both pledged to assist the hospital in bringing the neighborhood back to life.

The turnaround was relatively rapid. Plaza Court and surrounding properties are now a bustling mix of restaurants, shops, salons, offices and housing. The area is home to both a YMCA and a bowling alley.

When I first started covering the plight of the neighborhood and the uncertain future of its anchor, about half of the hospital's patients were traveling from out-of-town.

If they exited from Interstate 235 onto NW 10, they passed the Packard Building at Robinson Avenue, which was then largely empty except for Pat's Lounge with a storefront consisting of plywood painted blue.

If they were exiting the old alignment of Interstate 40 onto Walker Avenue, they passed another stretch of vacant commercial properties and empty lots before crossing NW 10, the gateway to St. Anthony.

A walk to some of the doctor's offices, meanwhile, might have required passing the Brass Lantern Inn, which in 2003 drew 33 calls for police and where at least one slaying transpired.

Those views now are entirely different. The motel is gone, the flop houses were transformed by Midtown Renaissance Group into upscale apartments. Several hundred apartments have been built from the ground up, including The Edge and Lift.

People feel safe walking, jogging and strolling with their children through Midtown.

When civic leaders rallied around St. Anthony, the hospital pledged to invest $220 million in rebuilding the campus established by Catholic nuns in 1898.

The recent completion of a new emergency room and ambulatory wing for the hospital marks the completion of that plan. The hospital campus is no longer a mishmash of medical buildings built around seedy motels and boarded-up storefronts.

The whole story seems like a commonsense outcome for Midtown. But in 2003, the city came very close to seeing a different direction for the hospital â€” one that would have left Midtown with a giant, abandoned cluster of buildings that might have doomed the neighborhood to a blight that would have taken years to recover.

The $220 million master plan changed everything for Midtown, and while it's done, the area's transformation continues. Both Midtown and the hospital will continue to evolve. Several developers have joined with St. Anthony in turning Midtown into an exciting mixed-use urban neighborhood.

But have no doubt: Without St. Anthony, you don't have Midtown.

After heart and liver transplant, OKC man appreciates life in new way

Published: April 23, 2017 12:00 AM CDT

Jim Sharrock wasn't enthusiastic about the doctor's recommendation.

For 40 years, Sharrock had lived with a condition that caused his heart's wall to be thicker than normal.

And throughout his adult life, physicians repeatedly told him that, most likely, he wouldn't ever need a heart transplant.

But, at 67, there he was, at Baylor University Medical Center in Dallas, with a cardiologist telling him the opposite.

"What if we just waited until things got worse?" Sharrock asked her.

"I don't think you have very long," the physician said.

"How long?" Sharrock asked.

"Less than a year," she said.

That got Sharrock's attention.

"Well â€” OK," he said.

And with that, Sharrock soon was placed on the organ transplant waiting list.

Sharrock originally was hesitant about the surgery because he would receive not only a new heart but also a new liver.

Over time, Sharrock's failing heart had placed too much pressure on his liver, and, as a result, he needed both organ transplants to ensure his odds of survival, and beyond that, a better quality of life.

"Ninety-five percent of the people who saw me, the first thing they said was, 'You look great' and then (they had) this big smile," the 69-year-old Oklahoma City attorney said.

Getting a diagnosis

In 1975, Sharrock went to the doctor for a physical exam as part of a requirement for a life insurance policy that he wanted to buy. He didn't know that visit would be so memorable.

During the visit, the doctor asked Sharrock, then 27, if anyone had ever told him that he had a heart murmur.

Throughout adolescence and early adulthood, Sharrock had been healthy. He played sports and was in the military. And as a part of that, he had undergone several physical exams. No one had ever mentioned a heart murmur.

Soon, Sharrock was diagnosed with what's now known as hypertrophic cardiomyopathy, a condition in which the heart muscle enlarges and thickens without an obvious cause, according to the National Heart, Lung and Blood Institute.

Essentially, the wall between the lower chambers of Sharrock's heart was thicker than normal.

Often, the first symptom of hypertrophic cardiomyopathy is sudden death, a term Sharrock heard an unsettlingly high number of times in the early days of having the disorder.

For years, he took medicine and lived a healthy life.

In 1993, though, he developed an atrial flutter, a type of abnormal heartbeat.

Over the next several years, Sharrock's heart would go out of rhythm, and usually, his body could fix it naturally.

However, over the time, the fluttering â€” which felt like a small bird was flying around in his chest â€” worsened.

Sharrock had two procedures to create scar tissues inside and outside the heart.

The first was a catheter ablation in 2002. His physician went into the interior of Sharrock's heart through a vein and used various types of energy to burn specific areas of the right atrium.

During the second procedure, his physician and medical team made several small incisions in Sharrock's chest and, using small cameras, lights and wires, they burned the top part of his left atrium.

But that didn't solve Sharrock's heart issues.

In 2008, Sharrock realized he had congestive heart failure. As a result, his body retained fluid, and he didn't have much energy to do anything physical.

"2008 was the hardest year of my life, with the possible exception of last year," Sharrock said. "Because in 2008, I was getting worse. There didn't seem to be a solution. ... I really just did not have much hope."

His doctors operated again, this time implanting a defibrillator that also served as a pacemaker.

Yet again, things went well for Sharrock â€” for a little while.

In 2015, he started retaining a noticeable amount of fluid, mainly in his abdomen. Sharrock's doctor soon diagnosed him with a nonalcoholic form of cirrhosis of the liver.

At this point, Sharrock didn't know what solutions were left.

In September of that year, Sharrock went to his fraternity's pledge class reunion at the University of Oklahoma.

There, he talked with a fraternity brother who was a cardiologist in Texas. Sharrock updated his friend on his health issues.

After the reunion, the friend called and asked him more questions about his liver issues.

The friend recommended that Sharrock try to get on the heart transplant list as soon as he could. He told Sharrock that his liver problems likely were related to his heart. Sharrock would soon find out his friend was right.

Getting the call

Next, a family friend recommended that Sharrock go to Baylor University Medical Center at Dallas, which has an experienced transplant team.

In late 2015, Sharrock and his wife, Nancy, went to the appointment at Baylor. They originally thought it would be a brief consult, but the doctor told Sharrock that he needed to be hospitalized. He was sicker than he realized.

Sharrock soon was told that he needed the dual transplant, a procedure that Baylor offers.

Dr. Shelley Hall, chief of transplant cardiology at Baylor, said when she saw Sharrock, she knew he wasn't in immediate danger, but his condition was only going to worsen.

In her 20-year career, Hall has been a part of six heart-liver surgeries. They're fairly rare, she said.

However, overall, Sharrock was healthy, and the team was optimistic that he would do well post-transplants.

"He was healthy â€” that sounds kind of funny when you're talking about somebody who needs transplant â€” but that's one of the nuances of transplants," Hall said.

"Their organ has to be sick enough to need it, but their body has to be well enough to go through it."

Sharrock was placed on the organ list Dec. 18. At that point, he and Nancy chose to move to Dallas, temporarily. They weren't sure how long it would take to get a match.

"Through most of that time, we were thinking, 'Gee, is this match going to happen?' " Sharrock said. "... We never really had time to worry very much because we got the call on Feb. 18."

Sharrock's surgery took 12 hours. First, the heart team performed its transplant, and then the liver team came in. After the liver team finished, the heart team returned to close Sharrock.

Life after surgery

Sharrock didn't wake up for three days after surgery.

And he didn't immediately feel better. His doctors pointed out that Sharrock went through two of the most traumatic surgeries at the same time. It would take awhile.

However, once he was on his feet, those feet took Sharrock back to the golf course.

Over the past year, Sharrock has restarted activities that he enjoys, including a regular golf game with friends.

Overall, Sharrock says he has become a more tolerant person, especially toward people who seem to be experiencing health issues.

He can remember not long ago when he was struggling to keep up with his family at football games and on trips.

Sharrock is grateful that, throughout his transplant experience, he was able to have hope â€” hope that he would be healthy again and able to do the things he loves. He's keenly aware that, for many people who suffer major medical issues, regaining a healthy life is often not imaginable or possible.

The transplants changed everything for him.

"That puts me in a position where my life expectancy is pretty much what it would be for a normal person my age, so for me, it's real positive," Sharrock said. "... Even when you get a bypass, and you go, 'Man, I've dodged a bullet there,' but you still know you've got heart disease. I don't have heart disease any more. ... They say a heart transplant is not a cure, but I haven't quite figured out why they say that."

"It's a small school, but you get every opportunity as a student," said Paige Williams, vice president of the school's board and a 2003 graduate. "You have choir, robotics, all the sports and just all these opportunities that didn't exist when I was there in school."

Williams, who joined the board in 2013, said the school's turnaround benefit from a team of people and a renewed commitment from the school's community.

"But her passion and commitment is what I point to in making The Mount into what it has become," Williams said about DeNegri.

What Mount St. Mary has become is a school tightly connected to its past, while rapidly moving toward a new identity.

"We had to let go of the way things used to be, but we needed to move forward while still holding onto the traditions," DeNegri said. "It was almost like we needed to get to know ourselves again and figure out where we wanted to get to."
