Google Drive, OneDrive, Dropbox, or iCloud?

August 12, 2015 by Natalie Tarp

The increasing data needs of businesses has fueled a search for an alternative way to share data with collaborators, clients, customers, and partners. Many businesses are using more data than they ever have before, and hard drives are falling behind in their capacity to store. Even though hard drive sizes have been increasing and cost per unit of storage has been decreasing, cloud services have proven to be a more flexible and robust way to store data. People are disposing their USB thumb drives and experimenting with the cloud, a powerful solution to both storage and sharing of content. There are four main cloud options for a business: Dropbox, Google Drive, iCloud, and Microsoft’s OneDrive.

The first question that any business will ask when thinking about a new technology is the cost. Google Drive and OneDrive both offer 15 GB of storage for free at the outset, while iCloud offers 5 GB and Dropbox offers 2 GB. There is a clear hierarchy of free storage. If your business only plans to share and store documents and a few pictures, this may be enough. But, for example, a design or engineering firm that needs to share images and models will need more. Each service has a monthly subscription for additional storage. Google Drive, Dropbox, and OneDrive all have the same pricing, $9.99 per month for a terabyte (1 TB) of storage. OneDrive also includes a subscription to the MS Office Suite. On the other hand, Apple’s iCloud charges $19.99 for the same storage capacity.

Each service is designed to bring users into the ecosystem of their parent company. Thus, iCloud functions on OSX computers and iOS devices, while Google Drive performs best when combined with Gmail and Google Docs. OneDrive is integrated into Office and works seamlessly with Windows. Only Dropbox is not tied to any other services. So, the value your business gets from a cloud service in part depends on the software that you already use. Heavy reliance on Office makes OneDrive more appealing, while using a lot of Google products pushes you towards Google Drive. If your business runs on Apple hardware then iCloud is likely to be a good option due to how easily it works across both computers and mobile devices in the Apple ecosystem.

Out of these four core services, none stands out as the best because they all depend on the business’s needs and existing tech. They all provide sharing and storage and easy to use interfaces, so none of them are lacking in basic features. If your company has high-end data needs as well as computing needs, there is an additional tier of cloud service solutions, most notably Microsoft Azure and Amazon Web Services. They offer advanced analytical tools and massive computing resources for working with big data. Most business won’t need these tools and can satisfy their needs with one of the four main cloud services previously mentioned.

In the end, the main choice may come down to whether you need a paid subscription or a free account. Google Drive and OneDrive offer more free storage than the other two cloud providers mentioned.  If your storage needs is increasing, then it is a good time to think about a paid subscription. Think about your business needs and analyze the different software’s your business utilizes on a daily base. This will help you determine which of the major four cloud services is best for your business. The cloud is the future of storage, and local storage will become less relevant for many applications as time goes on.
