Jessamyn of Mynmakes marks a book for folding in her Utah home.
Mynmakes uses antique Reader's Digest Condensed books that come in a variety of colors and styles.
The book pages are folded, rather than cut, maintaining the integrity and readability of the novel.

Artist creating 3D art from old books and selling it across the country

Many older bookworms grew up anticipating the bi-monthly Reader’s Digest Condensed Books, enjoying the abridged “cleaned-up” versions of the day’s best sellers. But now, those volumes fill thrift stores and garage sales all over the country.

Jessamyn Thurston has hit on a part-time business repurposing these old classics.

Thurston is an avid do-it-yourselfer, and last year she was looking around for a special wedding gift for her little sister. She hit upon a place doing folded book art, but their prices were way too expensive for her tastes, so she decided to try it out herself.

That first book took the longest, but it just might be the most important. She started designing and folding the book weeks before her sister’s wedding, and was still folding it on the airplane as she flew to the wedding. A woman in the seat next to her watched her progress.

“She finally asked me what I was doing, so I explained it to her. She turned out to be an investment banker who needed corporate gifts. She asked if I could do an order for her, and she was my first sale,” Thurston said.

From that first sale, Thurston passed her 100th sale on Etsy in June 2015, and still fills corporate orders as well. Thurston has streamlined her process ten-fold since that first order, though.

Thurston doesn’t destroy the old classics. She simply folds the top and bottom edges in a specific pattern, creating a stylized word like, “Love,” “Peace,” or “Home,” that can be seen as the book stands open with its pages fanned out.

Another part of improving her process involved her husband. From that first book that took a week, she now can take a book from design to finish in an afternoon.

“My genius husband wrote an app for me,” Thurston said.

When she first started out last year, she’d create her designs in Photoshop, and then count the pixels by hand and convert them to centimeters by calculator. She then had a spreadsheet with those measurements, and she went line by line, page by page. If she lost her place, or had to stop, there were many times she had to go back and count her pages to remember where she left off.

“It took me up to 10 hours to do a book. But Dylan’s program cut that in half. A lot of online sellers who do this have a week lead time. Now I can get a custom order, design it and fold it, and get it shipped out to them within two days — just like Amazon,” Thurston said.

The one part of her process that has remained fairly constant over this past year is her ingenious shoebox folding station. Held together with duct tape and adorned with pieces of old child-locks and popsicle sticks, the station doesn’t look like much, but it works perfectly. She can easily view the page she is on and where she needs to make her folds from the attached tablet, and can make those folds with precision using a potter’s tool. She can take the shoebox and a book anywhere, and has — folding books at her children’s soccer games, the doctor’s office, even in the car on road trips.

She’s been selling her books online at her MynMakes Etsy shop for a year and has seen some solid success. Though she could do it full-time, she won’t pursue that right now because she is a full-time mom to her two elementary-aged children. She homeschools them, and the “school” part of their Orem home also houses her book-folding production table.

“I could go big. I could do targeted advertising and up my sales, but that’s in the future. I’m a mom. I already have a full-time job right now,” Thurston said.
