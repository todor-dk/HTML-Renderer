    Snapchat: A New Mobile Challenge for Storytelling

    Talya Minsberg
    May 18, 2015 5:45 am May 18, 2015 5:45 am

    Talya Minsberg is a social editor for The Times.

    In early 2015, The New York Times started quietly experimenting on Snapchat, a mobile app that has been getting a whole lot of attention lately (and not just from teenagers).

    In late 2013, Snapchat began allowing users to create “stories,” a collection of videos and photos, complete with filters and the ability to add text and drawing to your creations. Each part of the story — each individual photo and video clip — lasts 24 hours, so you have to catch the story on Snapchat before it disappears.

    Envisioning what The New York Times could look like on Snapchat was tricky. How could a traditional media company fit into a medium known for bad selfies?

    For starters, once you really take a look at Snapchat, you realize that most of your preconceived notions of the mobile app are false. There are really creative individuals and organizations making some pretty compelling stories on Snapchat, and watching a story on Snapchat is less passive than you may think. When you are watching a story, you have to hold your finger down on the screen.

    While defining an objectively good Snapchat story is nearly impossible, the best Snapchat stories generally are ones that tell a narrative in a personal, visual way that pulls in and keeps the viewer. So naturally, we decided to reach out to the best storytellers we could think of, journalists at The Times, to see if they were up for a new mobile challenge. They were.

    Each journalist takes the account for a specified amount of time to tell a story from his or her vantage point. Videographers, reporters, photographers, editors and even an Op-Ed columnist have taken the account to tell their own story in the unique Snapchat style.

    By its very nature, Snapchat stories are both personal and ephemeral. Snapchat stories present a somewhat unfiltered view of people’s lives. Unlike with Instagram, Facebook or Twitter, photos and videos must be posted immediately, so there is no drafts folder or photo editing.

    But even Snapchat stories must uphold the same standards as anything else published by The Times. There are just more doodles and emojis in a Snapchat story than you would see in print!

    Here is an example of a recent Snapchat story, downloaded from Snapchat and uploaded to YouTube, created by a news assistant and web producer, Valeriya Safronova, at the Met Gala.

    “What’s great about Snapchat is that it’s a casual and playful medium,” Ms. Safronova said. “Images have short life spans and viewers look through them very quickly, so there’s no need to carefully stage a photo or video.”

    “Candid moments are prized above staged shots, and I saw plenty of those happening on the red carpet, such as when Madonna playfully berated her date or when Anne Hathaway joked about wearing sneakers into the dinner.”

    As we continue to experiment with Snapchat in new ways, we would like to hear what you think. Follow us on Snapchat by searching our username: thenytimes.
