"Getting rid" of a programming language is a massive undertaking when that language is woven in multiple services that make up a large company's product offerings. For LinkedIn, Scala is fundamental to many of their offerings, including their enterprise bread and butter products.

Many of the platform services and "mid-tier" API layers at LinkedIn are written in Scala, and certainly could not be replaced overnight.

But yes, over time, LinkedIn will be deprecating Scala usage & support.

Source: Worked at LinkedIn.

We are not getting rid of Scala at LinkedIn.  We've recently made the decision to minimize our dependence on Scala in our next generation front end infrastructure which is rolling out this year.  We've also made a decision to focus our development infrastructure efforts on Java 8, Javascript, Objective-C, Swift, C++, and Python given the nature of things that we are building right now and will be building in the foreseeable future.  That said, we have a ton of Scala code running in production, and will continue to provide basic support for Scala and those Scala systems for the foreseeable future.

It would be a reasonable business and engineering decision simply because the economic cost of both supporting Java and Scala is greater than Java alone.  I am sure it is a painful, costly and carefully thought out decision but in the end less is more.   Many technologies for a wide variety of reasons fail to gain economic traction.  I always choose my languages carefully with this is mind placing early winning bets on C, C++ and Java being lucky to avoid any losers.

It is difficult for Scala or any other new contender to displace the existing technology incumbent.  Over the decades many fashionable trends have died on the vine as the years passed.  Critical mass is a bitch to achieve and those that fail fall into dustbin sooner or later (Ada, lisp, prolog, smalltalk, forth, countless others).

A key factor is having a large established base of knowledgable engineers to draw from in the job marketplace.  Attracting talent is often what matters in the end not the actual language itself.  Being incrementally better is not sufficient to win the race.

For instance I have always been surprised at how technically backwards the Apple development platform has always been.  Objective C is a technically inferior language yet the huge Apple commercial presence makes it viable.
