On Hymnals and Screens

I recently stumbled on Tim Challies' blog titled "What we lost when we lost our Hymnals." 

I work at a big church that plays loud music.  In fact, I work in the Creative Arts department, right alongside a team of musicians actively writing new music for use in worship services.

And I miss my Hymn books.  Oh, how I miss the simple quietness of a simple, quiet service.

There are reasons for this, and not one of them made Mr. Challies' list. And while he and I seem to agree on our favorite style of worship service, I strongly disagree with his premise that "we" have "lost" anything by forsaking hymn books.

But before we get too deep, let's talk a moment about what Hymns are, and what they are not.

Back in my public school choir class, I was assigned a research project about a type of music and I chose Hymns.  I was surprised to learn that many Hymns were poems put to melodies that were familiar to the masses, such as drinking songs sung in bars.  This was done so that people would have little difficulty singing along, even if they were new to the faith.   Take a look at your beloved Hymnbook.  You may see the name of the author of the lyrics and under it the phase "Traditional Irish Tune" or something similar. This is code for "popular, common tune" or bar song.

In the places where many of these hymns originated, they didn't have hymn books either, and they needed a way for the whole congregation to keep up.

To give you cultural context, this would be like taking a favorite Talyor Swift or Beyonce song and writing new lyrics that reflect scripture or biblical truths.  I wonder what our "pro - hymnal" crowd would think if all of our worship music Sunday morning sounded like Billboards top 100 with new lyrics?

Is this true of all "Hymns"?  Of course not.  Some beloved classics are original songs.  And they do still speak to some people today (A great example: It is well with my soul).

But as amazing as these songs are, they are just that: songs.   Some may contain scripture and truth, but they are not often simply the scriptures put to music.  And when they are scriptures put to music, the scripture itself is the only thing sacred, the melody is simply a matter of preference.  

So with that in mind - back to Mr. Challies' point.  That "We" have "lost" things by having songs projected on powerpoint.

"We" is a big word. And while Mr. Challies may be mourning the loss of the hymnbooks I take strong exception to the idea that the Global Body of Christ has "lost" by reading lyrics on a screen in most American Churches.

I'll take his arguments point by point:

1. "We lost a deep knowledge of our songs. ...As we add new songs with greater regularity, we sing old songs with less frequency. This reduces our familiarity with our songs so that today we have far fewer of them fixed in our minds and hearts. "

 He assumes that everyone in the room would be familiar with any songs sung by the church.  It also assumes that singing the same 10 songs each week would "fix them in our minds and hearts."  The songs that are fixed in my mind and heart are the ones I listen to all day, every day.  They are contemporary music with christian themes, some worship songs.  After 10 years attending a church that used the same Hymnal, and had the same favorites, they were not fixed in my heart and mind.  And I grew up in the church.  A newcomer doesn't have a prayer.   If anything, this would become an argument for singing the worshipful contemporary songs from the radio on Sunday morning.  Not, I think, Mr. Challies' point. 

 2. "We lost the ability to do harmonies."  Yes.  Our culture is less musical now than it was in whatever past time Mr. Challies want's to conserve, I suppose.  But music is not a priority for public education, leaving music education a privilege of the financially secure.  Can we stop equating "financially stable" with "holy"?  Additionally, go back to the origins of the Hymns.  Many came from a time where harmonizing was not a priority.  Appeal to the common man was the priority....again, not something I see Mr. Challies advocating.

 3. "We lost the ability to sing skillfully." See above. 

 4. "We lost the ability to have the songs in our homes." Well, this is just categorically false.  My home is full of worship and music with Christian themes, most of which was never printed in a hymnbook.   Sad that Mr. Challies' family doesn't sing along to the radio or enjoy singing more contemporary music.  

 And once again - what is a new believer supposed to do with this?  They will be totally unfamiliar with the music, possibly untrained in music (see point 2), and further, unfamiliar with the 100+-year-old version of English many hymns are written in.   And inadvertently Mr. Challies has suggested that the best Christian families worship together by singing these particular songs.  Of COURSE, this is not his intention, but if someone was intimidated by this, could this expectation be a stumbling block for them in establishing family worship?  

 One song he points to  that he says would be difficult to sing as a family: "Oceans." It's vastly singable.  The trick is to not get hung up on the measures of instrumental music.  And in case you are wondering, you don't need to be a musician to know how to do that.  Ask any 12-year-old singing her favorite pop song in the park. She'll explain it to you.  

 But more importantly, go to the link and look at the comments.  This song is touching people deeply.  It's meeting them right where they are and leading them in worship to the one true God.  What is the point of music if not that?

 "We" have not lost anything by singing songs projected on a screen instead of printed in a book.   Some long time, older Christians, or old souls like myself, have been pushed outside of our comfort zones for the sake of others and should take this opportunity to examine our hearts as to why we feel so strongly that something is "right." 

 And I hope you agree, that if my discomfort can lead even one to the Kingdom, then let it be so.  And, dear Lord, please bring more discomfort.

Sunday, January 29, 2017

Don't worry, there is a bunny.

If you haven't heard Jim Gaffigan's take on Easter - go listen to it now.  It's 29 seconds.

Go. Right now.

I love Jesus and I love Easter.

And when you put it that way - it's absolutely insane.

I know, I know, all kinds of symbolism either originated these traditions and then were forgotten or have been superimposed onto them so we can feel good about celebrating the single most important event in history with neon yellow marshmallows in the shape of chicks.

But on its face - it is insane.

And this is where I am.  I was able to breathe through the campaign contributors in the cabinet, the sloppy transition, the flurry of Executive Orders and even "alternative facts."  Because frankly - as offensive as it all is none of it is new.  

And really, the immigration policies he's crafted with all the help of those smart people I was promised he'd surround himself with, are not new.  They are variations on a theme that has played throughout history time and again.

And they are as insane as those darn peeps.  They were then, they are now.

But unlike all the other hysteria about all the rich people who get to hold relatively meaningless appointed positions- lives are at stake.  Real lives, of real people, who did more to keep me safe than my current President would ever be willing to do.  Of defenseless children.  Of average people just like me praying for the safety of their family.

These are people and all those people who are dying.  Or living in conditions under which, for some of us, death might be preferable.

And this I find outrageous.

Set aside my mandate as a follower of Christ to love my enemy (Matt. 5:44) and then a handy-dandy little description in 1 Corinthians 13 about what love looks like.

On its face, this is bad public policy poorly executed.  Ask Senator John McCain or Congressman Justin Amash who tackle two good reasons that it's bad policy.  Or if you have more time - zip on over to This American Life, episode 607 and ask yourself how well the American Army will do in foreign conflicts without trustworthy translators.   Because after this kind of incredible support - who do you think would ever help us again??!

As for the poor execution?

Ask this kid.

But don't worry, there is a bunny.

Thursday, December 15, 2016

A confession.

My departure from the Republican Party was very loud on social media.

Like borderline obnoxious.  (Some might say actually obnoxious - I'll let you decide for yourself)

There is a reason for this.

Back when I thought there wasn't a snowball's chance in *&^$ Donald Trump would win the Presidency I pretty much kept my mouth shut about him.  This good little Republican was focused on unity at the expense of all else.  So when someone said "I like Trump" I'd smile, nod, and move on.

When I think back to all of those times I passed on an opportunity to change someone's mind, or at least ask them to reconsider, I cringe.

I was knowledgeable.  I'm reasonably articulate.  I have a (very small, and shrinking) sphere of influence.  And I squandered it.

But I'm not alone.  There are thousands, millions like me out there in both parties who for whatever reason *let* this happen.  The party elite's who let Hillary call in her chips and thus nominated one of the most unlikeable candidates in modern history.  The party elites who didn't fight harder against the fringe and let someone more unlikable (and more untrustworthy, amazingly) than Hillary Clinton win the party's nomination.

Reasonable moderates, Republicans and Democrats - we own this.  We let this happen.  Every time we threw our hands up in the air to say, "Never mind, do what you want" - we brought this to pass.

Or worse - when we let ourselves join the echo chamber - taking the cheap shot instead of asking the hard questions.  Trading this victory for future credibility.

But there was one group who did the unpardonable.  Everyone of you who gave in for politically expediency, you know who you are.  And I know who many of you are too.  Shame. on. you.

Well.

It happened.  And, here's the good news.  Just as I was wrong about his chances of being President, he's already started breaking all of the promises he made to his voters, so there is a decent chance I will be wrong about the apocalyptic nature of his Presidency.

We all may live to fight another day.

But this time - we need to fight.  And fight better.

Wednesday, August 3, 2016

On the last taboo.

I was probably in the 10th or 11th grade when, for one of my science classes, I did a report on Nystagmus.  It's a big word that means one's eyes dart back and forth nearly imperceptibly rapidly.

I was diagnosed at 3 months old.

Because it was my normal - and frankly one of a laundry list of issues I have with my vision, it didn't mean much to me.  It was a fun factoid to share in a friendly game of two truths and a lie.

I don't remember specifically what the assignment was or even doing it.  What I remember is sitting in the kitchen, home alone, on websites about Nystagmus on the red iMac and for the first time in my life hearing someone else tell me about what I experienced every day.

Suddenly there was a language for things like why I HATED THE STUPID TILE STAIRS at my High School and why when I would go into some stores I would be able to "see" but not see what was going on.

I remember my  mom walking in the door seeing me on the floor of the kitchen sobbing.  Like Seriously sobbing.  Because there were other people out there just like me. I've never met anyone else with Nystagmus.

While we are confident I was born with it - no one else in our family has the condition (although you better believe we all watched my son's eyes closely when they were born).

Somehow reading about the experiences of others in the message boards, testimonies and support groups lent some validity to the things I suspected were different but never talked about.  And even though I've never really felt the need to dive back into that research, as I've faced trials driving or when I have to explain to a standard optometrist how to examine my uncooperative eyes I've had comfort in not feeling quite so alone in that reality.

We all love knowing we aren't alone.  There is great comfort in a trial when someone says "Yes!  I know exactly what you mean!" Or you hear your own story in the words of another.

'Validating' isn't a strong enough word for the relief that pours over a soul when someone on the outside can say "Yes - what you feel is real."

Nystagmus isn't the last taboo.

Marriage challenges are.

The #Loveyourspoucechallenge is a beautiful thing!  And the older the marriage (and generally the older the pictures) the more fun I've been having seeing love from "back in the day" to "today."

It gives me hope :)

But if you have Nystagmus, I give you: I Cannot in Good Conscience Participate in the Love Your Spouce Challenge, written by someone I'd love to meet in real life :)

And if that bothers you - well.  Maybe this isn't your support group.  Move along and post another adorable picture.  I look forward to seeing it :)

Wednesday, May 4, 2016

My Hillary Moment

It's not widely known that Hillary Clinton was once a Republican.  She was a Goldwater Girl, and even chair of the Wellesley Young Republicans.  Then somewhere in the late 60's when President Nixon was running for office, she changed parties.

I don't know if it was Nixon himself who prompted her switch or another future President who came into her life at that time.

But in my imagination Hillary Clinton was in her early twenties, on the precipice of a political career looking into the face of the future of her party and just said "Nope."

No I'm not coming out in support of Hillary Clinton.  Yet.

But last night, like my imagined 20something Hillary in 1960something, I looked at the face of the standard bearer of the Republican Party and said "Nope."

I fought this moment.  Through the entire election cycle I have fought against Donald Trump.

And today, sitting here, having lost big, my only regret is that I didn't do more.

I should have quit sooner.  I should have said more when I had the captive audience.  I should have found a way.

But the voters have spoken, and they say that Donald Trump is what it means to be a Republican.  If  you agree - good for you.

But as far as I'm concerned, I'm out.

Friday, February 26, 2016

What if I'm not wrong.

It's the middle of a sunny afternoon when the big black truck comes down the road.

Maybe it's a bus.  

It doesn't matter.  

The next sound to reach my ears is the screaming and crying.  A women - begging, no, no, please don't take us.  A child, maybe 5 or 6.  Maybe loaded on the bus with mom, maybe taken by a social worker because the detention center is probably not suited for children.  A man and maybe an older child silently comply with the officer's instructions.  

No time to pack anything - the things they've acquired in the last 2, 5, 10, 15, 25 years will all sit in that house until the landlord figures out what to do.  Does he sell it?  Toss it?  Leave it - maybe they'll be back.  

The house sits vacant.  My son asks where did his friend go.  

And the stories will start to make the news - the conditions in the detention centers.  The realities of "guilty until proven innocent."  Maybe they are ok, maybe not - but better safe than sorry.  A 15 year old former good student, American born, killed when the only way to support himself there was to get involved with the drug lords.   People who've been here 25 years, plus or minus, killing themselves because what else are going to do?  Wait how many years for the system to "let" you come back to your life?  And what life will you be returning to?

And then there is the rest of us.  Because what would you say when your neighbor comes to you "We had no choice, we had to go to the ER and now they know.  Maybe if we stay in your basement?  What if you at least keep my children for awhile.  We think we can get back and they have birth certificates - they can stay in school.  I just don't know what will happen when they come for us.  You are white - no one will suspect"  

At least at first they won't.  Gotta wonder what the penalties will be when they start to figure it out. 

So in the middle of the afternoon you watch "justice" happen in your front yard.  

That's what it looks like.  That's how it will be.   There is simply no way to rip over 11 million people from the fabric of our society without some serious damage - collateral and otherwise.  

Funny.  11 million is the number who were sacrificed the last time a political leader convinced roughly 35% of his people he could make his country great again.  

You can say I'm being dramatic.  You can say I'm wrong - it couldn't possibly happen that way.  

But here's my question: what if I'm not?

Wednesday, February 10, 2016

Some sense in a senseless election

Yesterday, as I went about my day changing diapers, responding to emails, attending meetings, and wiping spit up off of my shirt, I kept saying out loud:

"We need to remember to vote tomorrow!"

I have a history of being slightly interested (no laughing please) in the political process so to forget to vote would be... well... rather shocking.  

AND as the daughter of a woman who won an election by drawing the word "elected" from a coffee can after tying her opponent...you get the idea - I gotta remember to vote.  I just gotta.

Fortunately, in the world of social media- Facebook has me covered.   All anyone is talking about today is the election.  And while the whole world seems to agree one should vote, there are several different types of "go vote" comments and statuses.

You have the folks who are sure that "IF YOU DONT VOTE ALL OF YOUR FREEDOM WILL GO AWAY FOREVER." Generally Republicans.  Also the Democratic version: "STOP THE EVIL REPUBLICANS"  They are in all caps because they are FREAKING OUT!!!.

Your typical: "I voted!" either on its own or with a description of all the other things that person did.  Also in this category are the good natured "Remember to vote today :)" (The smilie is optional.  Sometimes it's a patriotic picture) Generally moderates.

You have *my* people.  The people I'm about to betray by forgetting to vote.  Due to my past life there are many of these people in my news feed.  They are running on caffeine and adrenaline and comparing how many yard signs they got out last night and how many people they called today to remind them to vote.  (Listen, any of you reading this - please call me.)

Finally you have the cynics.  My favorite so far: "Trying to pick my favorite politician is like trying to pick which STD is just right for me."  These are funny.  They are a great way to blow off some steam.  But frankly, "politicians" aren't why you are frustrated with America.

Politicians are people who generally get involved after their own frustration reaches a critical level where they feel the only way they can possibly improve the world is by throwing themselves at the mercy of the voters and lay bear their entire lives for examination, critique and sometimes mean spirited criticism.  Are their ones who are drawn by the allure of power?  Absolutely.

But the same reality that ultimately crushes the spirits of the good ones also prevents the bad ones from being satisfied in their quest for domination.  The system is just way too big for any one person to change it.

Just ask the President.  He can tell you.

Elections matter.  Not because any one person is going to change the course of history, but because you are selecting the people you want to go and make decisions on your behalf.  So you want to choose reasonable responsible people who will generally view the world in much the same way you do.

But, let's stop pretending that they can change the entire course of human events.  They can't.  I've read the end of the story and *spoiler alert*, God wins.

Let's also stop pretending that we are always picking the lesser of "two evils".  Chances are pretty good that neither of the people on the ballot are actually evil.  They are people, with limited power (very limited, trust me) and generally they work so much harder than you can possibly imagine to try to make things just a tiny bit better.  And they do it in a time where absolutely everything they do and say is available to be ripped apart.  24 hours per day.  7 days a week.   Mostly by people who are either too entrenched in their own opinions to listen to another view, or by news outlets looking to make money.

These are people we are voting for.  They can't save you.  They can't destroy you.  All they can do is spend our money and write some laws.  So choose wisely, choose responsibly and vote Republican  (Sorry, old habits die hard)
