Golden moment for Beezers

Journey back in time: Brian Martin, left, Fred Rist, centre, and Norman Vanhouse with the Maudes Trophy they won for BSA in 1952 by riding across Europe on their 500cc A7 Star Twins 

12:00AM BST 04 May 2002

Comments

Independent testing ain't what it used to be, at least not since the demise of the Maudes Trophy, writes Michael Jackson. This Auto Cycle Union-administered prize, one of the toughest tests of a motorcycle, required a manufacturer to use randomly chosen parts from an assembly line to build a production bike. After a brief inspection, the machines were ridden across Europe, taking part in competitions, tests and time trails, and ridden back to the factory. There were failures galore.

Legendary British manufacturer BSA won the event for the first time in 1926 and also scored a memorable triumph in 1952.

That win by the Birmingham factory will be commemorated at the 13th Louis Vuitton Classic at the Hurlingham Club in London on June 8, when Norman Vanhouse will be reunited with his winning 500cc, BSA A7 Star Twin.

Fifty years ago, Vanhouse, along with Brian Martin and Fred Rist, rode their BSA twins from the factory on a 5,000-mile blast through northern Europe. In the gruelling International Six Day Trial in Austria, they were the only entrants to score gold medals for clean sheets. This was followed by 80mph, flying quarter-mile tests in Norway. Incredibly, the trouble-free Beezers were ridden back to the factory to collect the trophy.

The Maudes Trophy BSA model normally lives at the National Motorcycle Museum in Solihull, but it will be entered into the Louis Vuitton Classic along with 24 other machines in two classes; Class of 52 and Best Of The Last.
