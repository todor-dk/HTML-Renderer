PornoTube, Xpeeps

What's This?
By Pete Cashmore2007-01-12 01:49:35 UTC

Note: this post deals with adult social networking - skip over it if you're not interested.

Aside from CES, the other big conference in Las Vegas this week is full of popular startups that rarely get coverage in the mainstream press. Companies at the AVN Adult Entertainment Expo are talking about how to put an erotic spin on popular social sites like MySpace, YouTube and Metacafe. We used to think that pornography led innovation, but judging by the startups on display, it seems that the porn industry is more interested in making sex-related clones of existing sites.

xpeeps

Among the attendees is North Carolina-based xpeeps.com, a porn version of MySpace that launched in beta eight months ago - the official launch is this week, says spokesman Daniel Fairchild. The site includes chat rooms, forums and a job listings section for the adult entertainment industry. I can't claim to have ventured into the site, but it seems technically poor - many ready-made scripts look more professional.

PornoTube

Why would xpeeps' creators bother with original ideas when they can roll out more porn clones? A few months after creating the "MySpace of Porn", they launched PornoTube, the "YouTube of Porn". The founders point out that Pornotube is now among the top 20 sites on the web (by Alexa, presumably), meaning that it may have grown even faster than YouTube itself. Like YouTube, it also owes some of its success to controversial videos: the Kevin Federline and Britney Spears tape was reported to have appeared on the site late last year, generating more brand awareness. Here's betting that Daniella Cicerelli has also made an appearance.

YouTube's strict policy on adult content provided the perfect opportunity for PornoTube, but MySpace is more lax when it comes to monitoring the site: that would explain why xpeeps didn't get the same traction as its sister site. YouTube created a similar market gap by deleting clips of war and violence: LiveLeak is capitalizing on that opportunity.

Other Attendees

Not to be confused with the social search engine of the same name, Wink claims to be an online reality TV show with an adult edge (couldn't find a URL), while MovieMonster and U-Rent both claim to be the "Netflix of porn". Another attendee, Yappo, launched its video-on-demand service at the expo.

MySpace, a Place for Porn

If you're a MySpace user, you've no doubt noticed that the site is a haven for all kinds of marketers. In particular, the adult industry sees it as one of the Internet's most viral marketing and distribution channels. A company called Relaxx Entertainment was reportedly at the conference promoting its "MySpace Invaders" CD, an interactive porn CD that encourages viewers to learn more about the actors by visiting their MySpace pages and adding them as friends. In fact, software like Badder Adder has long since been used to add thousands of friends to a MySpace profile, and seems popular with everyone from bands to adult entertainment companies.

The Porn Stigma

The question for startups, of course, is whether you're prepared to risk your reputation by launching a site in the adult market. PornoTube made success look very easy, but VCs don't seem to look favorably on those companies that allow this type of content. That said, some of the most well-known video sharing sites have included adult content in the past, and some continue to do so. Guba was a porn haven until they decided to spin off their porn unit in early 2006. Their traffic fell as a result, and the founder has since left. Meanwhile, sites like Metacafe and LiveVideo use family filters to hide adult content from those who wish to avoid it - some claim that Metacafe owes much of its success to this edgier content. It seems likely that Flurl.com, recently acquired by the MySpace founder, also relies on adult material.

It's pretty easy to imagine porn clones that could be successful: Stickam, Digg and the rest, but the question is whether founders are prepared to risk their careers for the perceived opportunities. Another point worth emphasizing: it seems that in the social networking world, pornographers are trailing legitimate startups, rather than pioneering a new path.
