That's Ridiculous!

After hell froze over, we got this letter from Ryanair

By Jennifer Finger | August 16th, 2016

Antoinette and Thomas Brinkman wrote us to request assistance following a booking problem with Ryanair. They had booked tickets through Opodo, a European online travel booking website, to fly from Pisa to Palermo, Italy, on Ryanair, only to find themselves and their two children prevented from boarding the flight.

At the time of the Brinkmans’ flight, travel agents, including Opodo, were not authorized to sell tickets on Ryanair. Opodo had booked their tickets in the wrong names, used a company credit card to pay for the tickets rather than the Brinkmans’ credit card, and failed to pay baggage fees for two additional pieces of luggage that the Brinkmans wanted to add to their reservation.

Their case raised this issue: Who was responsible for the errors in customer service that forced them to pay an additional $1,000 to travel at the last minute? Was it the Brinkmans, Opodo, or Ryanair?

Our advocates contacted Opodo on behalf of the Brinkmans, because the online agency had failed to correctly enter the family’s booking in Ryanair’s system and charged the wrong credit card for the tickets and luggage fees (probably because they were unauthorized to make bookings on Ryanair at that time). The Brinkmans’ concierge was unable to print their boarding passes before they needed to depart for the Pisa airport for their flight. Opodo refunded the Brinkmans the name change fee of $203 and issued them a refund of $216 as a goodwill gesture.

The Brinkmans felt that they were due a refund of the price differential between what they had originally paid for their reservations and what Ryanair charged them for the replacement tickets, boarding passes, and luggage fees, which were priced in British pounds. The price differential is based on the exchange rates between the British pound and the euro on the day they booked their reservations and the day of their flight, when they had to pay again for tickets and luggage fees. They asked us to advocate with Ryanair to refund the $655 they believed they were still due.

Ryanair provided a very rare written response:

We regret to inform you that we have no record of any prior correspondence regarding this matter.

Firstly, we would like to stress that we do not recommend that customer book through third parties. We recommend they book directly with ourselves in order to ensure that they receive the best price and to avoid any confusion. We have no relationship with Opodo and bear no responsibility for their errors.

With regard to your complaint about the airport check-in fee, please be advised that all customers are required to check in online and print their boarding passes prior to arriving at the airport. An email reminding customers to check-in online and print their boarding passes is sent before each booked flight. If a customer does not check-in online, despite our repeated communications, then an airport check-in fee is applied.

We note that you arrived at the airport without having checked in and the charge was applied correctly. Please note that all customers are advised and reminded to check-in online and the requirement appears in our Terms and Conditions which are agreed to at the time of purchase. We bear no responsibility where customers engage a third party to make a booking on their behalf and as a result fails to comply with the Terms and Conditions.

With regard to required name change and the fee applied, we would like to point out our Terms and Conditions state that, while customers may request amendments of names prior to travel, a change fee will be levied on this service. The names on the booking originally were the names provided to Ryanair.

With regard to the baggage fees paid at the airport, this was the correct amount. Had you wished to add these to the booking and pay before attending at the airport, it was possible to do so. We note that you had difficulties due to using a third party to make the booking. As stated above, we do not recommend booking through third parties.


In conclusion, the problems experienced by you originated with the third party used by you to make the booking. Given these problems originated with the Opodo and are not the fault of Ryanair, I regret that I cannot accede to your refund request as we bear no liability in this case.

We regret that we cannot be of any further assistance and we hope to have the pleasure of welcoming you on–board another Ryanair flight again soon.

So Ryanair is refusing to accept any responsibility for the Brinkmans’ situation because they chose to book through a third party.

According to Ryanair’s contract of carriage, it charged the Brinkmans the extra fees for their tickets, boarding passes, and luggage because it had not received “the applicable fares, taxes, fees or charges” from the Brinkmans. The contract of carriage also provides that:

Fares, taxes, fees and charges are payable in the currency of the country of origin of travel unless another currency is indicated by us at or before the time payment is made, for example, because of the non-convertibility of the local currency. We may at our discretion accept payment in another currency.

Ryanair guarantees the currency exchange rate at the time of booking the ticket. This exchange rate will not change. If you do not choose Ryanair’s currency exchange rate you may be exposed to negative currency fluctuations between the time you make your booking and when your card issuing bank converts the transaction, as in most cases the currency conversion occurs a number of days after the date of booking the booking.

So had the Brinkmans chosen Ryanair’s currency exchange rate, Ryanair would have locked in the price of their tickets and luggage fees. Opodo, not being authorized to make bookings on Ryanair, would not have been authorized to offer that option to its customers.

On the one hand, Ryanair correctly claims that it’s not responsible for Opodo’s booking errors, the Brinkmans’ inability to print their boarding passes before arriving at the airport or requiring the Brinkmans to pay the remaining unpaid fees for their additional luggage when they tried to check in for their flights.

On the other hand, even though Ryanair doesn’t want its passengers to make bookings using third parties, such as Opodo, it should assist passengers with booking issues in order to provide good customer service, rather than brushing them off as “not its responsibility.” And giving passengers the brush-off has resulted in Ryanair receiving the dubious distinction of being the “most reviled airline in Europe.”

We also heard from Opodo, which claims:

I’m also sorry to say that Ryanair are [sic] being deliberately misleading in the letter…. For all of the millions of bookings we serve in Europe each year, individual airlines remain responsible for check-ins, the printing and costs associated with boarding cards, baggage fees and the customer experience at airports.

It looks to be these issues that Ms. Brinkman experienced problems with, not the original booking through our website.

A small minority of airlines — Ryanair included — are trying but failing to cling on to an older world, standing in the way of consumers increasingly using comparison sites like ours to get the best deal and the most convenient combination of flights. This could be the reason for the stance outlined in the letter.

Our advocates have determined that there isn’t anything more we can do for the Brinkmans. But we can issue a warning: If you’re planning to fly on Ryanair, don’t make your bookings through a third party — and make sure that Ryanair correctly processes your reservation. We also provide company contacts for Ryanair on our website.

As far as Ryanair is concerned, let the flyer beware.
